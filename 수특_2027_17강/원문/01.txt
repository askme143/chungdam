These efforts are hard to replicate; and to be able to do
deep work, we need a distraction-free environment.
 For many people though, we spend far too much time on the
other end of the scale, doing what is referred to as shallow
work.
Deep work is when you perform an activity with deep
concentration that pushes your cognitive capabilities, and by
doing so create new value and improve your skills. This style of working is not cognitively demanding but is
rather an execution of logical-type tasks that rarely create
new value (although they may still be important).
 We often perform these tasks while we are distracted.
 Shallow work has its place, both because routine-type
tasks need to be done and because we cannot spend our entire
time doing deep work. The problem with too much shallow
work is that we feel busy, maybe even stressed, but we rarely
see any output reflecting our experienced effort.
