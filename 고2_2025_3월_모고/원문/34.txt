Centralized, formal rules can facilitate productive activity by
establishing roles and practices.
The rules of baseball don’t just regulate the behavior of
the players; they determine the behavior that constitutes
playing the game.
Rules do not prevent people from playing baseball; they
create the very practice that allows people to play baseball.
A score of music imposes rules, but it also creates a
pattern of conduct that enables people to produce music.
Legal rules that enable the formation of corporations, that
enable the use of wills and trusts, that create negotiable
instruments, and that establish the practice of contracting
all make practices that create new opportunities for individuals.
And we have legal rules that establish roles individuals
play within the legal system, such as judges, trustees,
partners, and guardians.
True, the legal rules that establish these roles constrain
the behavior of individuals who occupy them, but rules
also create the roles themselves.
Without them an individual would not have the opportunity
to occupy the role.
